# data-governance-lifecycle

**Group:** 20-data  ·  **Runs as:** subagent: ../.claude/agents/data-architect.md  ·  **Mode:** audit + design  ·  **Default model:** sonnet

## Purpose

Govern data over its full lifecycle — ownership, stewardship, lineage, classification (esp. PII/PHI/PCI/financial), retention, archival, and lawful erasure — so the design is **compliant by construction**, not retrofitted at audit time. Answers: *for every store under a compliance regime, who owns the data, how long is it kept, how is it erased on request, and is every regulated field classified before it enters a collection?*

## Inputs & preconditions

- From `project-architecture-config.md`: the existing stack and its data stores (per `project-architecture-config.md`), compliance regime (PII and/or financial data; sector-specific obligations; RBAC tenant/org-scoping — per `project-architecture-config.md`), constraints (stack lock, no new datastore without a driver-justified ADR), and the `architecture/<slug>/` artifact location.
- The architecture **drivers**: the prioritized quality-attribute scenarios (utility tree) from `workflows/00-drivers/quality-attribute-scenarios.md` and `architecture/<slug>/quality-attributes.md` — every governance decision traces here. Compliance scenarios (e.g. GDPR right-to-erasure, data-minimization mandate, audit-log retention, PII residency) must appear as named QAS leaves or be surfaced as open driver questions; do not invent retention windows or regulatory obligations that are not stated in the BRD/PRD.
- The **data model** output from `workflows/20-data/data-modeling.md` — the aggregate inventory, the PII-classification table in `architecture/<slug>/threat-model.md`, and the collection-level schema. If data-modeling has not run, this flow reads the existing schema directly (per the schema/validator module paths named in `project-architecture-config.md`) and flags the missing classification table as a critical finding.
- The **domain analysis** output from `workflows/00-drivers/domain-analysis.md` — bounded-context ownership map is the basis for data-steward assignment; each bounded context must have one named owner role.
- **Preconditions:** `project-architecture-config.md` read; the utility tree (or stub) available; the existing schema and query patterns read from the codebase; the privacy-compliance architecture context from `workflows/40-cross-cutting/privacy-compliance-architecture.md` read if authored (LINDDUN threat model, residency driver, regime mapping). A flow with no identified compliance regime and no PII inventory downgrades to audit-only and says so.

## Oracle (source of truth)

The regulatory obligations applicable to the system (GDPR data-minimization + right-to-erasure + residency; HIPAA minimum-necessary + PHI safeguarding; PCI DSS data-retention/purge where financial card data is present; SOC 2 audit-log retention) expressed as **quality-attribute scenarios with measurable response measures** — e.g. "A data subject (source) submits an erasure request (stimulus) to the system (artifact) in normal operation (environment); the system erases or anonymizes all PII linked to the subject across all collections (response) within 30 days with a traceable audit event (response-measure)." Every governance decision — the classification scheme, the retention window, the erasure path, the lineage requirement — must trace to one of these scenarios or to a stated constraint. A governance rule with no traced compliance driver is a preference, not a hard finding.

- **hard:**
  - Every store under a compliance regime **classifies its data** using the four-tier scheme (public / internal / confidential / PII or PHI or PCI) at the field level; each field carrying subject PII or financial data is tagged. An unclassified-PII field in a regulated collection blocks.
  - Every classified store **states a retention window** (the number of days/years the data must be kept) and an **archival path** (where data moves at end of primary retention — cold store, anonymized snapshot, or hard delete) traced to a stated regulatory or business driver. A store with "retain indefinitely" and no traced driver blocks.
  - Every classified store has an **erasure path**: either a hard-delete procedure (with its cascading implications documented), a field-level anonymization procedure (`anonymized_at` + null/hash of PII fields), or an accepted-risk entry stating why full erasure is structurally impossible and what compensating control exists. No erasure path → blocks.
  - **No PII in logs or events without classification.** The audit-log store and any structured log or event emitted by the system must not carry raw PII fields (e.g. a subject's SSN, date-of-birth, bank account number) unless those fields are classified, their log-retention window is bounded, and an erasure path from the log store exists. Unclassified PII in logs is a critical finding.
  - **Lineage is traceable for regulated data.** For every regulated data entity (e.g. a core business record, an account record containing PII, a payment transaction), the lineage — the chain of systems/services that created, transformed, or read the data — is documented or structurally inferable from the audit log. A regulated entity with no lineage trail and no accepted-risk entry blocks.
  - **Data residency is honored.** Where a compliance driver states a residency constraint (e.g. data must remain in a specified jurisdiction), every store and every backup/replica path satisfies it or carries an explicit accepted-risk ADR. A residency constraint with no architecture response blocks.
  - **Data minimization is enforced by design.** Fields collected but not used by any named access pattern or business capability are flagged as over-collection candidates (traced to the GDPR data-minimization principle or the equivalent applicable obligation). An over-collection finding is a `major` hard finding where the field is PII; `minor` for non-PII.
  - Every significant governance decision (classification scheme, retention window, erasure strategy, lineage mechanism) is an ADR with context · ≥2 options · decision · consequences (positive AND negative), tracing `Driver refs` to a `QAS-*` in `architecture/<slug>/quality-attributes.md`. An ADR without alternatives or without negative consequences blocks.

- **soft:**
  - Governance is **designed in at modeling time** — classification happens when a field is added to the schema, not when a regulator asks. A design that requires a manual field-audit at erasure time scores lower than one that uses a PII-tagged schema layer or a data-class field registry.
  - **Data minimization is a first-class design principle**, not a cleanup pass. The model collects the minimum fields required to satisfy the functional scope; optional/enrichment fields are explicitly justified by a business capability.
  - The stewardship model is **operable**: data owner and data steward roles are concrete (named role or team, not "someone"), the governance policy is executable without heroics (a runnable erasure job, a bounded audit query, a scheduled archival task), and new regulated collections automatically inherit the classification policy (schema-level enforcement or a documented onboarding checklist).
  - The lineage design is **right-sized** — for a typical internal enterprise system (not a data warehouse or a streaming platform), full data-catalog tooling is likely accidental complexity; a structured audit log + a documented data-flow diagram is the right bar unless a driver demands more. Match the bar to the system's actual scale per `project-architecture-config.md`.

## Standards & techniques

- **Data ownership + stewardship** — every bounded context from `workflows/00-drivers/domain-analysis.md` has one **data owner** (the role accountable for the data's accuracy, access policy, and lifecycle decisions — e.g. "the Manager role owns the core-entity collection") and one **data steward** (the role responsible for day-to-day quality + classification maintenance). Ownership is not "the DBA" or "the team"; it is a named business role (per the project's RBAC roles in `project-architecture-config.md`) with an accountability that survives team changes.
- **Data lineage** — the chain of systems, services, and transformations that a data entity travels from ingestion to consumption. For example: a subject submits → an intake API creates a core record → a staff role updates it → a downstream process writes derived/financial data → archive → erasure. Lineage is documented as a data-flow diagram (Mermaid `flowchart` or C4 dynamic view) traceable to the audit log; not a narrative paragraph.
- **Data classification (four-tier scheme)** — Public (freely shareable) · Internal (business-sensitive, no regulatory obligation) · Confidential (trade secret / attorney-client, access-controlled) · Regulated (PII / PHI / PCI / financial — governed by an external regime). Within Regulated, sub-classify by regime. Each field in every collection carries a classification tag; the tag drives the retention window, the erasure requirement, the log-scrubbing rule, and the access-control tier.
- **Retention + archival policy** — retention is the period during which data is active (queryable at low latency); archival is the move to cold/compressed storage (accessible but not in the primary path); deletion is hard-purge. The policy per classification tier is: window length (in days or years, traced to a regulatory or business driver) + the archival mechanism + the hard-delete trigger. Illustrative example for a document store: TTL indexes enforce automatic expiry on time-bounded collections (e.g. an audit-log TTL); manual batch jobs handle PII erasure across linked collections; archival to an object-storage cold store or a compressed snapshot collection. Adapt the mechanism to the project's actual store per `project-architecture-config.md`.
- **GDPR right-to-erasure / data minimization** — right-to-erasure (Art. 17) requires that all PII linked to a data subject be erased or anonymized on a valid request within 30 days (or the applicable jurisdiction window) — this includes the primary collection, any denormalized copies, any log/event store, and any backup snapshot within the retention window. Data minimization (Art. 5(1)(c)) prohibits collecting more PII than is necessary for the stated purpose — the data-minimization check is an access-pattern audit: every PII field must map to a named functional requirement. These principles apply wherever GDPR applies; for non-EU jurisdictions, name the equivalent obligation or state "no erasure obligation" with the regulatory basis.
- **Six data-quality dimensions (DAMA DMBOK)** — Completeness · Uniqueness · Timeliness · Validity · Accuracy · Consistency. Prioritize the dimensions that matter to this system's domain (per `project-architecture-config.md` and the BRD/PRD) — for example: **Accuracy** (monetary or measurement fields must match source documents — financial/data integrity), **Completeness** (mandatory intake fields must be present before a core entity advances in its state machine), **Uniqueness** (a business identifier — e.g. an order/invoice number — is unique, enforced by a sequence-counter store; cross-reference `workflows/20-data/data-modeling.md`), and **Timeliness** (a stated latency driver from the BRD/PRD NFRs, e.g. a document-processing or extract latency budget). Violations of a high-priority dimension are findings with a traced QAS; low-priority dimensions surface as risks, not hard findings.
- **ATAM / utility tree** — governance options (field-level tagging vs document-level classification; TTL index vs batch job for retention; hard-delete vs anonymization for erasure) are scored against the utility tree (privacy compliance, operability, evolvability) using ATAM sensitivity and tradeoff points. The canonical tradeoff here is **compliance completeness ↔ operational complexity**: a fully automated, field-tagged, lifecycle-enforced governance design is compliant but operationally heavier; a lighter manual-procedure design is simpler but operationally fragile. Name the chosen point on this curve.
- **MADR** — every governance decision is an ADR (`shared/adr-template.md`): the classification scheme (a one-way-door decision — changing it after data exists requires a migration), the erasure strategy (hard-delete vs anonymization — a tradeoff between GDPR completeness and referential integrity in the audit log), and the lineage mechanism.

## Step sequence

- **audit:** Read `project-architecture-config.md` + the existing schema (per the schema/validator module paths named in `project-architecture-config.md`) + the audit-log store structure + any existing PII classification table in `architecture/<slug>/threat-model.md` → reconstruct the de-facto data inventory (every collection, its field set, and the regulatory regime it falls under) → check each collection against the hard oracles: every regulated field classified? every store has a retention window and archival path? every store has an erasure path? no PII in logs without classification? lineage traceable? residency honored? data minimization applied? ADRs present for significant governance decisions? → check the six data-quality dimensions against the utility tree (accuracy, completeness, uniqueness for the core business aggregate; timeliness against the stated BRD/PRD NFR) → emit findings + risks per `finding-schema.md`, grouped by `type:"compliance"` and `type:"data"` then severity (critical: unclassified PII in a regulated collection; unclassified PII in logs; no erasure path; no retention window for a regulated store — major: missing lineage, over-collection of PII fields, ADR incomplete for a one-way-door decision — minor: sub-optimal stewardship assignment, quality-dimension gap with low blast radius). Read-only, no artifact authored, no source edited.

- **design:** Frame (read `project-architecture-config.md` + utility tree + data-modeling artifacts + privacy-compliance context from `workflows/40-cross-cutting/privacy-compliance-architecture.md` if present; confirm the compliance regime mapping — the project's sector/PII obligations, applicable GDPR/state-privacy-law scope; surface open driver questions: retention window for the audit-log store, erasure window for subject PII, residency constraint if any, jurisdiction of data subjects; read-only) → Explore (≥2 divergent governance designs — e.g. Direction A: field-level PII tagging in schema + TTL indexes for log retention + anonymization-on-erasure job; Direction B: document-level classification flag + batch retention scan + hard-delete cascade; each as a concrete spec with its classification scheme, retention/archival/erasure mechanism, lineage artifact, stewardship model, and the six-dimension quality-gate list; read-only) → Evaluate (skeptical evaluator scores each option against the utility tree + hard oracles using ATAM; decision matrix: compliance-completeness · operational-complexity · evolvability × options; swap order to kill position bias; identifies sensitivity points — e.g. "anonymization-on-erasure is sensitive to whether the audit log captures the data-subject ID as a foreign key: if not, erasure is structurally incomplete"; identifies tradeoff points — e.g. "hard-delete cascade is a tradeoff point: it maximizes GDPR completeness but destroys referential integrity in the audit log, worsening forensic auditability"; picks winner and grafts superior ideas from runners-up; read-only) → Document (one writer — `data-architect` — authors: data-governance section in `architecture/<slug>/design-doc.md`; PII classification table updated in `architecture/<slug>/threat-model.md`; retention/archival/erasure policy table; data-flow lineage diagram as diagrams-as-code; stewardship model section; ADRs for the classification scheme, erasure strategy, and retention mechanism in `architecture/<slug>/adr/`) → Verify (fitness functions: PII-classification completeness — every field in every regulated collection has a classification tag or a documented exception; erasure-path coverage — every PII-tagged collection has an erasure path entry; retention-window completeness — every collection has a stated window or an accepted-risk entry; lineage-diagram compiles; ADR completeness — every one-way-door governance decision has a complete MADR ADR; no-PII-in-logs check — audit-log field inventory contains no raw PII fields; data-minimization check — every PII field maps to a named functional requirement or is flagged as over-collection; evaluator re-scores from artifacts) → loop ≤ 8 or pass.

## Assertions & exit gate

- Every collection under a compliance regime has a field-level PII classification table entry (no unclassified regulated field).
- Every regulated collection states a retention window traced to a compliance driver (BRD, stated NFR, or named regulation); "indefinite" with no driver blocks.
- Every PII-bearing collection has a named erasure path: hard-delete cascade, anonymization procedure, or an accepted-risk ADR stating why erasure is structurally constrained and what compensating control exists.
- No raw PII field appears in the audit-log store or any structured log/event output without classification, a bounded log-retention window, and an erasure path from the log store.
- Lineage for every regulated data entity is traceable: documented in a data-flow diagram (diagrams-as-code that compiles) and structurally supported by the audit-log schema.
- Data residency is either confirmed satisfied by the current deployment topology or an open driver question is raised for the controller to resolve — no silent assumption.
- Every PII field in the schema maps to a named functional requirement; fields with no functional mapping are flagged as over-collection candidates.
- The stewardship model names a concrete owner role per bounded context; no collection is ownerless.
- Every significant governance decision (classification scheme, erasure strategy, retention mechanism) is a complete MADR ADR: context · ≥2 options · decision · consequences positive AND negative, with `Driver refs` tracing to a `QAS-*` in `architecture/<slug>/quality-attributes.md`.
- The lineage diagram and the PII classification diagram both compile without error.
- **Gate:** hard oracles green (every regulated field classified; every store has retention window + erasure path; no unclassified PII in logs; lineage traceable; residency addressed; ADRs complete; diagrams compile) AND (design) rubric mean ≥ 0.8 (governance designed in at modeling time, data minimization honored, stewardship operable, lineage right-sized for this system).

## Output

Write `artifacts/data-governance-lifecycle/report.json` per `shared/report-format.md` — findings per `finding-schema.md` (`type:"compliance"` for unclassified PII / missing erasure path / unaddressed residency / no retention window for a regulated store; `type:"data"` for data-quality dimension gaps / lineage gaps / over-collection; `oracle:"hard"` for the blocking cases enumerated above; `oracle:"soft"` for stewardship design craft, lineage right-sizing, classification-at-modeling-time discipline); the verification block (fitness-function results, PII-classification coverage, erasure-path coverage, lineage-diagram-compile result, ADR completeness, data-minimization check, rubric score) for design mode; open driver questions listed explicitly (retention window for the audit-log store, erasure window for subject PII, residency constraint, applicable jurisdiction for GDPR/state-privacy law) for the controller to resolve — never silently assumed.

## Guardrails

Per `shared/guardrails.md`:

- **Propose & document — never write product code.** The data-governance flow authors ADRs, the PII classification table, the retention/erasure policy, and the lineage diagram. It does not write schema-level field validators, TTL-index creation commands, erasure-job scripts, or any file under `backend/**` / `frontend/**`. The controller implements the chosen governance design.
- **Ground every rule in a compliance driver.** No retention window without a named regulation or stated business driver. No erasure requirement without a traced QAS. A governance rule with no driver is a preference, not a finding — either tie it to a named obligation or drop it.
- **Do not invent regulatory obligations.** If the BRD/PRD does not state a retention window, a residency constraint, or a specific regulation, surface it as an open driver question — never assume "30 days" or "EU residency required." The controller resolves open driver questions; this flow does not guess them.
- **Cross-reference siblings, do not duplicate their oracles.** PII classification and LINDDUN threat model are co-owned with `workflows/40-cross-cutting/privacy-compliance-architecture.md` — reference it, do not re-derive it. Aggregate boundaries and consistency models are owned by `workflows/20-data/data-modeling.md` — add the governance columns (retention, erasure, residency) to the existing PII table rather than creating a parallel artifact. The six data-quality dimensions cross-reference the access-pattern inventory in `workflows/20-data/data-modeling.md`.
- **Record the tradeoff and the rejected alternatives** on every governance ADR. The canonical tradeoff for erasure strategy is compliance-completeness ↔ referential-integrity-in-the-audit-log (`shared/fallacies-and-tradeoffs.md` → Normalization ↔ Read performance analog); name the chosen point. A governance ADR with only upside is incomplete (`shared/adr-template.md`).
- **One writer preserves conceptual integrity.** The `data-architect` is the sole writer for the governance artifacts. Proposer and evaluator agents are read-only. A second writer (e.g. `adr-author`) runs only sequenced, never concurrent, on a non-overlapping artifact.
- **Non-destructive and safe.** Read-only on any live environment. Never run an erasure job, alter a collection, or drop a field against a real database. This flow produces the *erasure design* (the ADR, the anonymization procedure spec, the TTL-index proposal as a design artifact); the controller/operator applies it.
- **Flag one-way-door decisions explicitly.** The classification scheme (changing it after data exists requires a schema migration across every collection) and the erasure strategy (anonymization is irreversible; hard-delete is irreversible) are one-way-door decisions — mark them in their ADRs and apply the corresponding analysis depth.
