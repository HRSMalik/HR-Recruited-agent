# architecture-drivers

**Group:** 00-drivers  ·  **Runs as:** subagent: ../.claude/agents/requirements-analyst.md  ·  **Mode:** audit + design  ·  **Default model:** sonnet

## Purpose
Extract the architecturally-significant requirements (ASRs) — the functional scope, the constraints, and the quality attributes that actually shape structure — and separate them from the bulk of requirements that do not drive architecture. Answers: *which requirements are architecturally significant (technically risky, high business value, or hard to change), what constraints are non-negotiable, and what does the utility tree need to address?*

## Inputs & preconditions
- From `project-architecture-config.md`: the existing stack, deployment target, scale/SLO seed targets, compliance regime, team topology, constraints, and the locations of the architecture description, ADR log, and diagram source.
- The full requirements corpus: the BRD/PRD (`docs/`), `project-understanding.md` (confirmed workflows, roles, permissions, data flows), `docs/jira-ticket-map.md`, any existing NFR statements or SLAs. Read these before running — never derive requirements from memory or inference.
- Preconditions: the source-of-truth documents are available; a functional scope summary (what the system does and who uses it) is either stated or readable from the requirements corpus. If the requirements corpus is absent or ambiguous on a driver, surface it as an open question — do not invent an ASR or a constraint.
- **Bootstrap exception — no upstream quality-attribute scenario drivers:** This is the flow that *produces* the driver set; it therefore has no pre-existing utility tree or prioritized `QAS-*` inputs to reference. It bootstraps from the requirements corpus alone. Every downstream flow (starting with `workflows/00-drivers/quality-attribute-scenarios.md`) depends on this flow's output as its driver input. The scope of this run is accordingly downgraded to: read the corpus, classify every stated requirement, and surface every unstated driver as an explicit open question — the quality-attribute scenario response-measures are not yet available to evaluate against.

## Oracle (source of truth)
The requirements corpus (BRD/PRD/`project-understanding.md`) as the ground truth; the ASR classification criteria as the filter; and the forward-traceability rule as the structural check.

- **hard:** every requirement labeled an ASR is accompanied by an explicit justification for why it is architecturally significant — it must fall into at least one of: (a) *technically risky*: the team has low confidence a satisfactory solution exists; (b) *high business value*: a failure here defeats a primary business goal; (c) *cross-cutting*: it constrains multiple components or boundaries simultaneously; (d) *hard to change*: the decision is a one-way door (datastore, auth model, public API contract, deployment topology). A requirement that fails all four criteria is not an ASR — it is a functional detail, classified and set aside. Every ASR also traces **forward** to either a named quality-attribute scenario (as a leaf in the utility tree consumed by `workflows/00-drivers/quality-attribute-scenarios.md`) or a named constraint (as a row in `workflows/00-drivers/constraints-assumptions-risks.md`); an ASR with no forward trace is an orphan and blocks the gate.
- **soft:** the ASR set is **complete** (nothing structure-shaping is missing — the reviewer can name no omitted requirement that would alter a component boundary, a consistency model, or a cross-cutting control) and **lean** (no non-significant requirement inflates the architecture — no functional CRUD detail masquerades as an ASR; the set is the smallest set that fully constrains the design space).

## Standards & techniques
- **ASR identification** — the SEI's practice of separating architecturally-significant requirements from the mass of functional requirements: ASRs are the subset that simultaneously constrain the design space AND carry high consequence if violated. Use the four-criterion filter (technically risky / high business value / cross-cutting / hard to change) as the classification predicate. A requirement that is merely important to the business but is easily implemented by any architecture is a functional requirement — classify it as such.
- **Functional vs quality attributes** — functional requirements describe *what* the system does (create a claim, route to an adjuster, apply a payment); they rarely drive architecture on their own. Quality attributes (*how well* it does it — under what load, with what latency, at what availability, under what security constraint) are the primary architectural drivers because they impose structural decisions that cannot be added after the fact. The distinction is the first cut in any requirements-to-architecture mapping.
- **Constraints taxonomy** — constraints are non-negotiable boundaries on the solution space. Classify them in three bins: *technical* (stack lock, existing infrastructure, integration contracts that cannot be renegotiated), *business* (budget, timeline, team topology, build-vs-buy policy), *regulatory* (PII/HIPAA/insurance-domain rules, audit mandates, data-residency requirements). Constraints are not drivers to optimize against — they are fixed; the architecture must satisfy them, not trade against them.
- **ATAM / utility tree seed** — the ASR extraction here is the first step in the ATAM utility tree construction (`evaluation-method.md`): quality attributes bubble up from the ASRs; each quality attribute is then refined into prioritized 6-part scenarios with response measures in `workflows/00-drivers/quality-attribute-scenarios.md`. The output of this flow is the *raw material* for the utility tree — not the tree itself; that is the next flow's job.
- **ISO/IEC 25010** quality characteristic taxonomy — use as a completeness check on the quality-attribute dimension of the ASRs: functional suitability, performance efficiency, compatibility, usability, reliability, security, maintainability, portability. Walk each characteristic and ask whether the requirements corpus contains anything that constrains the architecture along that axis; if yes, it is an ASR candidate.
- **One-way-door test** (from `adr-template.md`) — any requirement whose satisfaction demands a decision that is expensive to reverse (the data model, the auth mechanism, the external-system integration protocol, the deployment topology) is an ASR by definition. Flag it explicitly; these drive the ADR queue.
- **Conway's Law as a constraint lens** — the team topology is a constraint that shapes what decompositions are viable. A decomposition that requires coordinating deploys across a team boundary that does not exist is a structural risk; surface it. An ASR that demands a service split the team cannot own is a risk, not a free choice.

## Step sequence
- **audit:** Read the requirements corpus (`docs/`, `project-understanding.md`, BRD/PRD) → enumerate all stated requirements → apply the four-criterion ASR filter to each → classify each requirement as: *ASR (quality-attribute)*, *ASR (constraint)*, or *functional (non-ASR)* → for each ASR, record the justification (which criterion / why architecturally significant) → check each ASR for a forward trace to a quality-attribute scenario or a named constraint → flag every ASR with no forward trace as a gap → check the constraint set for completeness across the three bins (technical / business / regulatory) → check ISO 25010 axis coverage (walk each characteristic; name any axis where the corpus is silent but the system's domain implies a constraint) → emit findings + risks (read-only, no artifact authored, no source edited); group findings by type then severity (critical → cosmetic); emit a separate risk register for all `type:"risk"` items with likelihood×impact per `finding-schema.md`.
- **design:** Frame (read the corpus, extract and classify every requirement — read-only) → Explore (apply the four criteria to each requirement; draft the full classification table including non-ASRs and the reason each was excluded) → Evaluate (separate skeptical evaluator reviews the proposed ASR set for completeness and leanness: any missing structure-shaping requirement? any non-significant requirement inflating the set? names the forward traces; checks the constraint bins; checks ISO 25010 coverage) → Document (ONE writer authors the ASR catalog into `architecture/<slug>/adr/0001-asr-catalog.md` using the MADR context block: each ASR has its id, statement, classification criterion, one-way-door flag, and forward trace to a `QAS-*` scenario or a named constraint; functional non-ASRs are listed as excluded with the reason) → Verify (traceability check: every ASR has a forward trace; every named constraint appears in `workflows/00-drivers/constraints-assumptions-risks.md`; every open driver question is logged as an explicit open item, not silently resolved; the one writer re-checks completeness and leanness; evaluator re-scores) → loop ≤ 3 or pass.

## Assertions & exit gate
- Every requirement in the corpus has been classified: *ASR (quality-attribute)*, *ASR (constraint)*, or *functional (non-ASR)*; no requirement is left unclassified.
- Every ASR carries a written justification referencing at least one of the four criteria (technically risky / high business value / cross-cutting / hard to change).
- Every ASR traces forward to either a named `QAS-*` scenario (input to `workflows/00-drivers/quality-attribute-scenarios.md`) or a named constraint row (input to `workflows/00-drivers/constraints-assumptions-risks.md`); zero orphan ASRs.
- Constraint coverage: at least one constraint from each of the three bins (technical / business / regulatory) is identified, or the absence of a bin is explicitly argued (not silently omitted).
- ISO 25010 walk: every quality characteristic where the domain implies a structural consequence has an ASR; any characteristic left silent is recorded as a deliberate exclusion with a reason — not a gap.
- Every one-way-door ASR is flagged; these drive the ADR queue for `workflows/70-documentation/architecture-decision-records.md`.
- Every requirement the team has not yet stated a target for (an unstated RTO, an unstated concurrency ceiling, an unstated data-residency rule) is recorded as an **open driver question** with the downstream impact if it remains unresolved — never silently assumed and never invented.
- **Gate:** hard oracles green (every ASR justified + forward-traced; every open driver question explicit; constraint bins covered) AND (design) evaluator rubric mean ≥ 0.8 on completeness and leanness.

## Output
Write `artifacts/architecture-drivers/report.json` per `shared/report-format.md` — findings + risks per `finding-schema.md` (`type:"driver"`, `oracle:"hard"` for an unjustified ASR, an orphan ASR with no forward trace, a silently-assumed open driver question, or a constraint bin gap; `type:"risk"` for any open driver question whose resolution is blocked or any Conway's-Law structural risk); the `drivers[]` block carries the classified ASR table (id, statement, criterion, forward-trace ref, one-way-door flag). Open driver questions are a named list in `notes[]` — each with the decision they block and the downstream risk if left open. Findings are grouped by type then severity (critical → cosmetic); `type:"risk"` items form a named **risk register** with likelihood×impact per `finding-schema.md` Audit output rules.

For design mode, the ONE writer authors the ASR catalog as `architecture/<slug>/adr/0001-asr-catalog.md` (MADR context block: each ASR + classification + forward trace; excluded non-ASRs listed with reasons) and writes the **verification block** into `report.json` — traceability coverage (% of ASRs with a forward trace), hard-oracle results (every assertion from the exit gate, pass/fail), and rubric score (evaluator mean on completeness and leanness, ≥ 0.8 to pass) per `shared/report-format.md`. This artifact is the primary input to:
- `workflows/00-drivers/quality-attribute-scenarios.md` — consumes the quality-attribute ASRs to build the utility tree (the 6-part scenario + response-measure layer)
- `workflows/00-drivers/constraints-assumptions-risks.md` — consumes the constraint ASRs and open driver questions for the RAID log
- `workflows/70-documentation/traceability-mapping.md` — the ASR catalog is the root of the driver → decision → component → fitness-function traceability matrix; no ASR here = a gap in the traceability chain

## Guardrails
Per `shared/guardrails.md`: propose and document, never write product code. The ASR catalog (`adr/0001-asr-catalog.md`) is an architecture artifact in `architecture/<slug>/`, not a source file. The requirements-analyst agent is **read-only** in audit mode (no `Edit`/`Write`); the ONE `solution-architect` writer authors the catalog in design mode — no other agent writes it. Ground every ASR in the stated requirements corpus (`docs/`, `project-understanding.md`): **never invent an ASR or a response measure**; if a driver is unstated, it is an open question, not an assumption. Record the tradeoffs of including vs excluding a requirement from the ASR set (a false positive inflates the architecture; a false negative leaves a structure-shaping concern unaddressed). The four-criterion filter is the external oracle — not the agent's judgment of what "seems important."
