# team-topology-decomposition

**Group:** 10-styles-decomposition · **Runs as:** subagent: ../../agents/solution-architect.md · **Mode:** audit + design · **Default model:** sonnet

## Purpose

Use **Team Topologies** (Skelton & Pais) and **cognitive load** as a first-class decomposition driver alongside Conway's law — so that every proposed bounded context or module boundary is traced not just to a business capability but to a team that can own it within its cognitive load budget. Answers: *given the org's team structure and cognitive load limits, which team type (stream-aligned / platform / enabling / complicated-subsystem) should own each part of the system, and does the proposed architecture's seam map fit — or does it require the inverse-Conway maneuver (reshaping the org to get the architecture we want)?* This flow consumes the boundary candidates from `decomposition-strategy.md` and the domain context map from `../00-drivers/domain-analysis.md` and overlays the team topology lens, so that no seam survives unless a real (or planned) team can hold it within cognitive load.

## Inputs & preconditions

- From `project-architecture-config.md`: the existing team topology (headcount, who owns which branch/component, and whether the project is currently **one stream-aligned team** or already split — treat a single small team as one stream-aligned team until a concrete driver demands splitting), the stack lock, deployment target, and the existing module inventory (e.g. auth/RBAC, the core domain workflow + intake, account/org onboarding, users, audit logging; any near-term planned contexts; any deferred integration seam), all as declared in `project-architecture-config.md`.
- The **drivers / utility tree** from `../00-drivers/quality-attribute-scenarios.md` — in particular any modifiability, team-autonomy, independent-deploy, or time-to-market scenarios that motivate team/boundary reorganization. A topology redesign with no driver (no team-scale pain, no cognitive-load evidence, no independent-deploy need) does not proceed — record the over-engineering risk and downgrade scope.
- The **domain model + context map** from `../00-drivers/domain-analysis.md` — the DDD bounded contexts and the core/supporting/generic classification. The bounded context is the candidate unit; this flow maps each to a team type and interaction mode, it does not re-derive the context map.
- The **boundary candidates** from `decomposition-strategy.md` — the set of module/service seams already validated against independent-deployability, capability-not-layer, acyclic dependencies, and driver-traced granularity. This flow adds the team-ownership and cognitive-load gate on top.
- The **team topology survey** — the actual team names, sizes (headcount), current ownership areas, cognitive load self-assessments (or observed overload signals: long cycle times, context-switching evidence, knowledge silos). If this is unavailable, surface it as an open driver question and do not assume an answer.
- Preconditions: `project-understanding.md` + `docs/` (BRD/PRD) read; the utility tree and the context map exist (or this flow surfaces the missing inputs as open questions rather than guessing); `decomposition-strategy.md` artifacts read.

## Oracle (source of truth)

The **Skelton & Pais Team Topologies model (2nd ed.)** — the four team types, three interaction modes, cognitive load limits, and the inverse-Conway maneuver — applied to the boundary candidates and the org's actual or planned team structure. A boundary is only right-sized if a single team can hold it within cognitive load and own the full delivery lifecycle; a boundary that straddles two teams or overloads one is wrong regardless of how clean it looks on a context map.

- **hard:**
  - **One owner per service / bounded context** — every proposed bounded context or module maps to **exactly one** owning team. A service straddles two teams if and only if two distinct teams each make independent changes to it — that is a hard failure (`type:"decomposition"`, `oracle:"hard"`). The test: draw an ownership matrix (rows = teams, cols = services/modules) — every column must have exactly one `O` (owner); multiple `O` in a column blocks.
  - **Cognitive load budget satisfied** — no team's assigned set of services/modules exceeds the team's cognitive load capacity. Operationalize as: a team of *N* developers can deeply own at most *C(N)* bounded contexts where *C(N)* is bounded by the Team Topologies heuristic (a team of 5–8 cannot hold more than ~3–4 complex domains simultaneously). If a team's assigned contexts exceed the estimate, flag it as a `critical hard` finding — the decomposition is wrong at that granularity for that team.
  - **Inverse-Conway compliance** — where the target architecture's seam requires a team boundary that does not yet exist (e.g. a dedicated platform team for shared infra, or an enabling team to lift stream teams off a hard problem), the design **explicitly names the inverse-Conway maneuver required**: what org change is needed, which new/split/merged team, and which existing work it absorbs. A design that proposes a platform service owned by "the whole team" has not applied the inverse-Conway maneuver — that is a hard failure (no named owner, cognitive load undefined).
  - **Interaction mode stated per team pair** — every pair of teams that interact must declare its mode: **collaboration** (working closely, temporary, suitable for exploration — not a steady-state; time-bounded), **X-as-a-Service** (one team consumes a well-defined API from another; the producer team owns the interface contract independently), or **facilitating** (one team uplifts another's capability, then steps back). A cross-team dependency with no declared interaction mode, or a *collaboration* mode applied to a steady-state relationship that should be X-as-a-Service, blocks (it masks coupling as teamwork). The interaction-mode declaration must appear per team-pair in the design artifact.
  - **Fracture planes align with team boundaries** — every proposed cut point traces to at least one of the Skelton & Pais fracture planes: business-domain subdomain, regulatory compliance boundary, user/customer geography, risk/change cadence, technology/runtime, performance isolation, or funding stream. A seam that matches no fracture plane has no structural justification — flag as hard (it will drift back to coupling under schedule pressure).

- **soft:**
  - Boundaries are **stable against the org's likely next growth step** — if headcount doubles from one to two stream-aligned teams, the seams land cleanly on the split line without a big reshuffle (fracture-plane alignment is the proxy for this).
  - **Platform and enabling teams are identified where they reduce stream-team cognitive load** — shared infra concerns (observability, auth, CI/CD tooling, the future Phase-2 agent runtime) that currently load the single stream team are called out as platform-team candidates, even if no such team exists today; the recommendation is ADR-shaped (a decision with a tradeoff, not a wish).
  - **Collaboration mode is time-bounded** — every collaboration-mode relationship carries a proposed exit criterion (the shared problem is solved, a service API is stable, the enabling team has transferred capability). An open-ended collaboration is a latent dependency — flag advisory.
  - **Team cognitive load is bounded** — the design does not add new domains to an already overloaded team. The heuristic (not a hard number absent self-assessment data) is used as an advisory signal, not a gate, when load data is unavailable.

## Standards & techniques

- **Team Topologies (Skelton & Pais, 2nd ed.)** — the four team types and their applicability:
  - *Stream-aligned team*: owns the full end-to-end delivery of a value stream (a business domain / customer journey); the default, primary pattern; should be unburdened of cognitive load by platform and enabling teams. For a project starting from a single small team: that cross-functional team is stream-aligned to the core platform (per `project-architecture-config.md`).
  - *Platform team*: provides internal services (self-service tooling, infra, shared capabilities) so stream teams don't carry platform cognitive load; interacts via **X-as-a-Service**. Candidate trigger: if shared-auth, observability, or a deferred integration seam (per `project-architecture-config.md`) grow past the single team's cognitive budget.
  - *Enabling team*: uplifts stream teams on a hard problem (new tech, DDD context-mapping, performance engineering) via **facilitating** mode, then exits; temporary.
  - *Complicated-subsystem team*: owns a component that requires deep specialist knowledge the stream team cannot maintain (e.g. a proprietary ML model, a regulated financial calculation engine); interacts via **X-as-a-Service** — the subsystem team sets the contract, the stream team consumes it. Trigger: a component whose complexity would overload any stream team's cognitive budget if embedded.
- **Three interaction modes** — collaboration (temporary, high-bandwidth, suitable for discovery/complex problems); X-as-a-Service (stable interface contract, low collaboration, the default steady-state); facilitating (enabling upskill, then steps back). The mode governs how teams' tools, APIs, and governance processes must be designed.
- **Team cognitive load** (Skelton & Pais, Ch. 4) — intrinsic (inherent to the domain), extraneous (coordination, tooling friction, cross-team asks), germane (productive learning). The decomposition must minimize extraneous load on stream teams. Cognitive load is the primary reason to introduce a platform or enabling team; never introduce them without a load signal.
- **Inverse-Conway maneuver** (Melvin Conway, restated in Team Topologies) — design the team structure to produce the system structure you want, not vice versa. If the target architecture requires a seam the current org cannot own, name the org change as a concrete recommendation (not a hope). Cross-reference `decomposition-strategy.md`'s Conway-alignment soft oracle — this flow makes Conway first-class and hard.
- **Fracture planes** (Skelton & Pais, Ch. 6) — the natural organizational and technical cut points: business subdomain, bounded context (DDD), regulatory/compliance regime, user persona/geography, change cadence (volatility), technology stack/runtime, performance isolation, funding stream. A good decomposition cuts along a fracture plane; a bad one cuts across one (the two halves will drift back together under schedule pressure).
- **Domain-analysis input** from `../00-drivers/domain-analysis.md` — the core/supporting/generic classification drives team-type assignment: core subdomains map to stream-aligned teams (they are the value-differentiating business domains the org must own deeply); supporting subdomains are candidates for platform or enabling teams; generic subdomains (commodity, off-the-shelf) should not consume a team at all — use a managed service / open-source and eliminate the cognitive load.
- **Quality-attribute rubric** (`../../shared/quality-attribute-rubric.md`) — the "team-aligned" and "evolvability" rubric dimensions are the soft-oracle scoring levers for this flow; the hard oracles above gate first.

## Step sequence

- **audit:** Frame the drivers → read `decomposition-strategy.md` artifacts + `../00-drivers/domain-analysis.md` context map + `project-architecture-config.md` team topology → build the **ownership matrix** (teams × services/modules; flag every column with multiple or zero owners as a hard finding) → for each team, estimate the cognitive load of its assigned set of contexts (using the Team Topologies heuristic + available self-assessment signals) and flag overloads as `critical hard` → check every cross-team dependency for a declared interaction mode (flag missing or permanently-collaboration-mode as hard) → verify each boundary against the fracture-plane list (flag any seam that matches no fracture plane as hard) → identify any inverse-Conway mismatch (a seam the current org cannot own without a structural change — flag as hard finding with the maneuver required) → emit findings + risk register grouped by `type:"decomposition"` then severity, read-only, authors no artifact, edits no source.

- **design:** **Frame** (confirm the utility tree — especially modifiability, team-autonomy, cognitive-load scenarios — and the context map; read-only) → **Explore** (`architecture-explorer` ×N produces divergent team-topology + boundary configurations, e.g.: (a) single stream-aligned team with no split — modular monolith, cognitive load within budget, Conway-trivially-satisfied; (b) stream-aligned team for the core domain workflow + platform team for shared-auth/observability as X-as-a-Service — inverse-Conway maneuver names the platform team; (c) stream-aligned team + complicated-subsystem team for a regulatory/financial calculation engine if that becomes a driver — each option a concrete spec with the ownership matrix, the interaction modes per team pair, the fracture planes each seam sits on, the cognitive load estimate per team, and the inverse-Conway maneuver required if any; read-only) → **Evaluate** (`architecture-evaluator` scores each vs the utility tree + the four hard oracles via ATAM; locates the sensitivity point — *team-topology complexity is sensitive to current headcount: a platform team at headcount ≤ 5 adds more coordination overhead than it removes* — and the tradeoff point — *X-as-a-Service reduces stream-team cognitive load but introduces an interface-contract negotiation cost and a dependency on the platform team's release cadence*; order-swapped to kill bias; the **simplest option that satisfies every hard oracle** scores highest — introducing team types the org cannot yet staff is an over-engineering risk to flag; picks a winner, grafts superior fracture-plane choices from runners-up) → **Document** (the ONE writer — `solution-architect` — authors the team-topology view as a text+diagram spec: the ownership matrix as a table, the interaction-mode declarations per team pair, the fracture-plane justification per seam, the inverse-Conway maneuver as a concrete org recommendation if required; **one ADR per significant team-boundary decision** with the rejected team configurations and their tradeoffs; folds into the `design-doc.md` as a team-topology section + a dedicated `team-topology.md`; reuses the existing boundary structure from `decomposition-strategy.md` — only documents where the team lens changes the seam, does not re-author the full decomposition) → **Verify** (the ownership-matrix completeness check as a hard gate — every service/module column has exactly one `O`; the cognitive-load estimate per team within budget; the interaction-mode declaration completeness; the fracture-plane coverage; diagram-compile; traceability — every team boundary ↔ fracture plane, every inverse-Conway maneuver ↔ named driver; the evaluator re-scores from the authored artifacts) → loop ≤ 10 or pass.

## Assertions & exit gate

- The **ownership matrix** is complete and non-overlapping: every proposed bounded context / module maps to exactly one owning team; no context has zero owners or multiple owners.
- Every team's assigned set of contexts is **within cognitive load budget** (Team Topologies heuristic applied; gaps where load data is unavailable are listed as open driver questions, not silently accepted).
- Every **cross-team interaction has a declared mode** (collaboration / X-as-a-Service / facilitating); no collaboration-mode relationship is open-ended (exit criterion stated or flagged as open).
- Every seam **traces to a named fracture plane** (business subdomain, bounded context, regulatory boundary, change cadence, technology, performance isolation, or funding stream); no seam matches zero fracture planes.
- Every place the target architecture requires a team boundary that does not yet exist has a **named inverse-Conway maneuver** — the org change recommended, the team type, and the driver that justifies the overhead of adding a new team.
- Every significant team-boundary decision has a **complete ADR** (context · ≥ 2 options · decision · consequences — positive and negative · the interaction-mode tradeoff · the cognitive-load tradeoff · rejected team configurations).
- Diagrams (the ownership-matrix view + the team-interaction diagram) **compile** (the diagram-compile hard oracle per `shared/diagramming-standards.md`).
- Every finding and every team-boundary recommendation traces a `driver_ref` to a quality-attribute scenario or constraint from the utility tree — no driver-less topology change.
- **Gate:** hard oracles green (one-owner-per-service · cognitive-load-within-budget · interaction-mode-declared · fracture-plane-traced · inverse-Conway-named-where-required · ADR-complete · diagrams-compile · driver-traced) AND (design) rubric mean ≥ 0.8 (boundary stability against org growth · cognitive-load reduction for stream teams · platform/enabling identification justified by load signal · interaction-mode appropriateness · conceptual integrity).

## Output

Write `artifacts/team-topology-decomposition/report.json` per `shared/report-format.md` — findings/risks per `finding-schema.md` (`type:"decomposition"`; `oracle:"hard"` for: a service with multiple or zero owners · a team overloaded past cognitive load budget · a cross-team dependency with no declared interaction mode · a permanently-collaboration-mode steady-state relationship · a seam matching no fracture plane · a missing inverse-Conway maneuver for a seam the org cannot own; `oracle:"soft"` for: open-ended collaboration without exit criterion · a platform team introduced without a cognitive-load signal · boundary stability risk against the org's next growth step), each carrying its `driver_ref` (the QAS or constraint it protects), `tradeoff` (what the team split costs — coordination overhead, interface-contract negotiation latency, platform team staffing — vs the cognitive-load reduction it buys), and `alternatives_rejected` (the team configurations considered and why not). For design mode, include the verification block (ownership-matrix completeness, cognitive-load estimates per team, interaction-mode coverage, fracture-plane coverage, hard-oracle results, rubric score). Authored artifacts land in **`architecture/<slug>/`** per `project-architecture-config.md`:
- `team-topology.md` — the ownership matrix, the team-type assignment per bounded context, the interaction-mode table per team pair, the fracture-plane justification per seam, the inverse-Conway maneuver(s) required.
- `adr/NNNN-*.md` — one MADR ADR per significant team-boundary decision.
- `diagrams/*.puml|*.md` — the team-topology view (ownership matrix as a table + a team-interaction diagram showing team types and interaction modes as diagrams-as-code).
- Findings and sensitivity/tradeoff points folded into `tradeoffs.md`; the cognitive-load fitness check specified in `fitness-functions.md`. Open driver questions (unstated headcount plan, unavailable cognitive-load self-assessment, unconfirmed platform-team staffing) listed explicitly for the controller — never silently assumed.

## Guardrails

Per `shared/guardrails.md`: **propose & document — never write product code** (this flow authors the team-topology view, the ownership matrix, the interaction-mode declarations, and ADRs; the controller restructures the org and the codebase). **Ground every team-boundary recommendation in a driver** — no team split a driver doesn't demand; introducing a platform or enabling team without a cognitive-load signal or a scale driver is résumé-driving and is flagged as an over-engineering risk (apply the right-size rule from `project-architecture-config.md`: a small team on a modular monolith defaults to the one-stream-aligned-team configuration until a concrete load or scale driver is evidenced). **Reuse the seam candidates from `decomposition-strategy.md`** — this flow adds the team-topology lens, it does not re-derive the domain seams from scratch. **One writer** preserves conceptual integrity of the team-topology view (no mixed team-type criteria, no seam that is capability-based in one place and team-accident-based in another). **Record the tradeoff and the rejected team configurations**, never just the winner — the interaction-mode chosen, the cognitive-load cost of the split, and the inverse-Conway maneuver overhead are half the deliverable. **Never recommend an org change without naming the driver** — Conway's law is a design tool, not a rationalization for a reorg. Cross-reference `decomposition-strategy.md` (the boundary candidates this flow applies the team lens to), `../00-drivers/domain-analysis.md` (the context map and core/supporting/generic classification that drives team-type assignment), `../../shared/quality-attribute-rubric.md` (the team-aligned soft oracle dimensions), and `../50-infrastructure/platform-engineering.md` (the platform team's service catalog and technical remit, consumed where a platform team is recommended).
